## **Columbia University’s Pre-College Programs & the Internship in Building Community**

### **History**

Columbia’s first Pre-College Program, “The Summer Program for High School Students,” began in 1986 and featured a single-session and a cohort of about 80 commuter students. Its first residential component took place in 1995. In 1999, in response to the growing demands of student needs and student life programming, the Internship in Building Community was instituted to provide a dedicated, specially trained residential life staff for the students.

In 2020, the program, now known as the Summer NYC Pre-College Program, welcomed nearly 2,700 students over three online program sessions. This was the first instance of large-scale, international online high school programming for Columbia University, being marked as a success in continuing to offer our students full classes and programming schedules throughout the COVID-19 Pandemic. In 2022, Columbia University reinstituted the on campus Summer NYC Pre-College Program while continuing to offer online options. 

Since our first cohort of approximately 40 interns, the Internship in Building Community has now grown to be a preeminent student affairs internship—with more than 1,500 alumni, drawing interns from across the USA and world—and will welcome about 125 “IBCers” in 2026.

### **Overview of Columbia University’s Pre-College Programs**

The Summer Pre-College Programs in New York City, previously the High School Summer Immersion Program, are the main focus of our work through the Internship in Building Community. Summer sessions are either three weeks or one week in duration. Students choose one curricular option only. The courses are non-credit courses but are college-level in difficulty. Courses typically meet for approximately two hours each morning and two hours every afternoon. The three-week sessions feature both commuter and residential students; the one-week session is commuter only. The IBC serves the residential and student life needs of all three sessions.

## **Staff Communication and Information**

A key definition of ‘authority’ in the IBC is that of ‘having command of information.’ It is important for interns to be able to answer questions about the program from students and others. Interns can access information from many sources, and it is up to them to be diligent in reading the training materials, the information made available to them during the program, and most of all, by asking lots of questions. Indeed, interns who ask lots of questions in turn have lots of answers.

  

Equally important is to be confident in your knowledge. Please do not make a decision or give information to a student from what you *think* is true. There is never harm in taking time to get the right answer from the right voice\! Also, please be patient if receiving a response takes some time; as developed as our program is, the program is always encountering new situations and learning too.

  

Please recognize that interns need to contribute to the information flow. When interns communicate what is happening to the Senior and Admin staff, then the leadership team can be responsive to the needs of the students and staff.

# **Basic Intern Responsibilities**

## **Residence Hall Presence**

While there is flexibility for individual schedules built into the internship role, there are still expectations for continued presence on the Morningside campus. All interns are expected to keep a strong presence in the residence halls. While this is clearly completed with responsibilities such as lounge shifts and curfew checks, interns are expected to regularly be present and engaged with the students in halls.

  

An intern’s main responsibility is to the safety and experience of our pre-college students. Interns are resources and guides, not only for program information, but to help each student reach their program goals during their stay with us. This includes being available to your students, familiarizing yourself with each of them, and offering them support. 

  

## **Shifts and Duty Assignments**

During the day, there are several types of shifts that occur such as lounge shifts, floater shifts, and program kiosk shifts, as detailed below. Interns will be expected to serve in at least two lounge/office shifts per week, along with 1-2 overnight duty shifts per week.

  

## **Lounge Shifts**

Each day, there will be a group of Resident Assistants (RAs) responsible for lounge shifts in the residence halls. These shifts will involve:

  - Greeting students as they enter and exit the building
  - Ensuring students do not exit after in-building curfew
  - Stopping students coming in after in-building curfew
  - Answering student questions about campus or program policies
  - Assisting students with Public Safety/building access issues 
  - Escorting facilities staff to student rooms/spaces
  - Helping students with lockouts
  - Maintaining the cleanliness of common area lobbies and lounges
  - Managing elevator loads and building noise
  - Generally overseeing the safety and security of the building
  - Working on building projects such as putting up/taking down flyers or bulletin boards as requested by an Resident Director (RD)

  

These shifts take place in the lobby of each building. When you arrive on shift you should report to your assigned station noted on the shift spreadsheet. Under no circumstances should you be napping, playing video games, or leaving the building (unless it's your official break). You should be dressed appropriately (i.e. not in pajamas). Keep your phone on because the office may contact you about lockouts or health forms. 

  

For issues that occur while on shift, please contact the Resident Director (RD)

for your building if it is before 5:00 pm. After 5:00 pm, please call the Resident Director  on duty. If your Resident Director is not available, please contact the Assistant Director of Student Affairs by cell phone. 

  

## **Floater Shifts**

During the day, there are several Resident Assistants (RAs) responsible for floater shifts. These RAs assist with all day-time program needs across campus. As a floater, when interns have not been given a direct task by admin, they are expected to be present in the Student Services Office.

  

Floaters will need to keep their phones on and await a call from staff who need support with a task. Some things that you may be asked to do on shift include: take students to the health center, pick up food from the cafeteria for sick students, run an errand for an administrator, cover a shift for someone else’s break, or provide additional help at a program event. 

  

If you get called on by multiple staff for task support, please follow the priority list below. You should communicate with the staff asking for help if you get called to complete a higher priority task.

  

Priority of Tasks:

1.  Student Health Center visit
2.  Task assigned by an administrator
3.  Break coverage
4.  Task assigned by a Coordinator or Resident Director (RD)

  

## **Breaks**

Staff are allotted one unpaid 30-minute break during their shifts. To take a 30-minute break, staff must coordinate with the other staff members on shift so that at least one staff member remains on-site at all times. Please make sure to reflect your break on your timesheet. Staff MUST take a break whenever they fulfill a formal shift. 

*For example, if you are scheduled 10:30AM - 3:00PM, you can take a thirty-minute break. Your total hours on your timesheet would be four hours.*

  

**While on shifts, staff** ***must*** **keep their phones on their person and be responsive to admin outreach.**

  

## **Curfew Check**

Each evening, interns will be responsible for checking each of their students in for curfew. **Resident Assistants (RAs) must see students face-to-face while confirming their presence. Students can not be checked in via text, group chat, or through another RA without approval from a supervisor.** RAs must stay in the hall during and between curfew checks. 

  

For in-building curfew, students should meet their RA at their designated meeting location to let them know they are in the building. After in-building curfew, students must remain in the building until the next morning. This is an important time to check-in with your students about their day and troubleshoot any issues they are having. This is also a good time to share announcements.

  

At in-room curfew, all students will be required to be in their assigned dorm rooms. This does not mean they are required to go to bed, but they do have to go into their rooms. Some students may opt in to go to bed early after a long day, however this does not excuse them from checking in for curfew. RAs should be knocking on each of their student’s doors to see that they are in their rooms at the designated times.  
  

If a student is under 30 minutes late to curfew, this goes in the [curfew tracker form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSf_b2ZMPi5QUsPNQexLpm0fSI1NuPQq6jFAWIq-iIxiF-NsGg/viewform?usp=sharing&ouid=112985824442366814188). After 30 minutes, an incident report will need to be completed and follow-up will occur with your building’s Resident Director.

  

In-Building Curfew: **\[RAs must be in-building to do curfew checks at this time\]**

  - Sunday-Thursday: 9:00 PM
  - Friday-Saturday: 10:00 PM

  

In-Room Curfew: **\[RAs are off after in-room checks are complete, unless on duty\]**

  - Sunday-Thursday: 11:00 PM
  - Friday-Saturday: 12:00 AM

  

## **Hall Coverage**

Unless it is your designated time off, all interns, including commuter and online RAs, are required to be in their designated residence hall between in-building curfew and in-room curfew each night. Interns must be visible and accessible during this time to help with emerging student and program needs. Interns cannot be in another part of campus or off-campus during this time, nor can they be in their room with the door closed.

  

## **Being On Duty / On Call**

Around twice per week, each intern will be required to be on duty. Being on duty involves being in the residence hall overnight to respond to emerging student needs and emergency situations. ***Duty is a shift from in-room curfew until morning lounge shift begins***. You must do the following:

  - Check-in with the Resident Director (RD) on duty to receive any special instructions for the evening.
  - Complete floor/building rounds:
      
      - 15 minutes after curfew
      - 45 hours after curfew
      - 75 minutes after curfew
  - Let the Senior Resident Assistant (SRA) know when you are done with rounds each time
  - Be sure to keep you ringer on and off of ‘Do Not Disturb' mode throughout the night so that you may respond to calls
  - In the morning, send an updated report **if any incidents came up in the middle of the night/early morning**
  - Submit your duty report for the night at 8:50 AM
  - All Maxient reports should be in before 9:00 AM the next morning

  

While on duty, you are not allowed to leave the building under any circumstances. Please pick up food or any items you need prior to in-building curfew. You may not leave the building, even to grab food from a delivery man. Leaving the building while on duty will result in a formal write-up with Human Resources. 

  

## **Keys: Lost, Sharing, Replacement, Master Keys**

You will obtain a Columbia ID Card on move-in day. Your CUID will grant you access to the campus, residence halls, Ferris Booth Dining Hall, Uris Hall, Lerner Student Center, and Dodge Fitness Center. Should you lose your card, the ID Office in Kent Hall will issue you a new one. After receiving a new card, you will need to visit Furnald Hall to have the ID card re-encoded with your housing access. Under no circumstances should you let anyone (fellow interns, staff or students) borrow your ID Card.

Verify that the student has their ID card in their room, or determine if they need an escort to Kent Hall during business hours if they have lost their ID. Once the student has been let back into their room, master keys should be *immediately* returned to the staff member storing it. 

  

If it is after hours or the weekend, please communicate with the Housing Coordinator on call to see if it is possible to get a temporary access card for the student. 

  

# **Intern Evaluation**

As the Internship in Building Community is a professional development opportunity, we will evaluate interns on the following competencies:

  - **Communication:** Clear and effective communicator. Able to successfully facilitate group dialogues, present or manage external communication, and contribute to discussions in a respectful and meaningful way. Communicates relevant information and updates to supervisors and colleagues in a timely manner. 
  - **Community Building:** Creates a positive and respectful environment for students. Takes initiative to address any issues or recognize a potential issue. Able to plan and execute all necessary programs. Created engagement opportunities in an inclusive way for all residents, and is consistently present in the hall and at events. Ethically uses programming funds and allocates the funds properly.  
  - **Planning:** Utilizes backwards planning to achieve goals and deliverables on schedule. Able to self manage, prioritize tasks, and plan work time accordingly. Utilizes technology or other tools to support time management. Anticipates possible challenges and makes plans to mitigate those issues.
  - **Collaboration:** Builds and maintains positive relationships with others. Balances their contributions on their team to ensure they are adequately supporting the overall output of the team. Works to build consensus with their team members and seeks to understand the perspective of other colleagues. 
  - **Professionalism**: Demonstrates ethical behavior and mature decision making. Respectful of team members, students, and other program stakeholders. Applies appropriate boundaries with students and colleagues.

# **Community Engagement**

## **Community Values and Mission Statements**

Students of our Pre-College Programs hold many different perspectives in respect to ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, nationality, culture, and gender identity. Diversity and inclusion are not only central to the student experience, they are key components to learning and development. As part of the staff charged with leading these students during their time at Columbia and as representatives of the greater Columbia community, it is imperative to provide experiences that allow students to capitalize on the rich education and growth found in the active exchange across multiple identities within a community.

Columbia Pre-College and Non-Degree Programs recognize that identity, social responsibility, allyship, and equity must be addressed at multiple, interconnected levels to best support students' various developmental stages. IBC interns provide programming, act as resources, and establish and maintain a supportive environment for personal identity development, mentorship, intercultural learning, social justice education, and institutional advocacy.

At the program level, much of this work is done by our Pre-College Student Affairs Team, providing cross-cultural training and awareness-building programming, helping our students take on leadership positions in a diverse world. Individual interns are encouraged to initiate their own programming ideas, which could include anything from a special interest student club to a subject-specific presentation.

## **Engagement & Outreach Framework**

The IBC’s approach to diversity and inclusion have been developed in conference with and mirrors the three-tiered approach of Columbia University’s Office of Multicultural Affairs.

**Intra-community** work focuses on constituency-based support and education, particularly for socially marginalized and underserved communities. Programming conceived and led by IBC interns explores aspects of personal identities, how those identities can manifest within and contribute to our community; the history of social oppression, its community impact, and the experiences and cultural heritage of groups. These projects facilitate a stronger understanding of self, a deeper sense of belonging, a destination for students to find those with similar community interests, closer connections within communities, and better support for communities, which have historically and socially been underserved.

**Inter-community** work focuses on cross-cultural dialogue and building bridges with others across communities, including programming and resources that encourage community education and collaboration among different communities and support the forming of ally groups. Intercultural communication and social justice education are critical components to this tier. As these programs emerge on our calendar, please inform your students about them and encourage their participation.

**Community Advancement** builds upon both the intra-community and inter-community tiers and speaks to the work to change individual, cultural, and institutional actions and policies that may discriminate or marginalize any group. We ask that interns consider their opportunity to positively impact the identity and community perspectives that the students hold, to actively engage the students in dialogues about community and identity, and to facilitate the building of a student community that the students may use as a reference point throughout their lives for how a socially just and inclusive community works.

## **General Safety Advice**

New York City is the safest large city in the United States. That said, in order to ensure that you have a safe and pleasant stay here, you and your charges should follow the guidelines below—and use common sense:

  

**Before each trip, remind students to:**

  

  - Bring a fully charged phone for emergency calls or texts.
  - Make sure students have their RA’s and Residence Life office phone numbers with them at all times. If you (or your students) ever feel unsafe or need help, call them. The program can help wherever you are.
  - Stay with the group at all times and in close proximity to chaperones.
  - Immediately inform group leaders of any discomfort, illness, or negative experiences.
  - Bring a water bottle and stay hydrated throughout the trip.
  - Be prepared to pay for their own metrocard. Students should purchase their MetroCards in advance of the trip so that there are no issues with purchasing on the day of the trip.
  - Communicate full trip plans, including meet-up locations in case of separation, before departing campus.
  - Coordinate to keep staff members at the front and rear of the group at all times
  - Implement a "buddy system" for students, especially in crowded areas or when brief separations are necessary (e.g., restroom breaks).
  - Establish a discrete signal or code word for students to alert staff of potentially dangerous situations.

  

**During the trip keep in mind:** 

  

  - Groups:
      
      - Travel in groups whenever possible, particularly in Morningside Park and Riverside Park.
      - When traveling in groups and/or with students, stay aware of strangers around you and others with you. Stay together and move to different areas or subway cars should any nearby stranger make the group uncomfortable.
      - Conduct regular head counts every few minutes to ensure all students are present.
      - Provide students with a phone number to call if separated from the group.
      - Actively check in with students throughout the trip to ensure they feel comfortable and address any concerns.
      - If the group has free time to explore a designated location, provide a clear return time and location. 

  
  

  - Awareness in NYC
      
      - Be aware of your surroundings and of where your wallet and valuables are, especially in crowds. In crowded subways, keep your wallet in your front pocket rather than the rear, and keep your purse closed and held in front of you or on the side.

  

  - Never leave backpacks or other belongings out of your sight, even on campus.  Even in the dorms, you should not leave valuables lying around.

  

  - Don't flaunt jewelry, cameras, or cash in public.
  - Encourage students to stay alert and limit phone use to remain aware of their surroundings.
  -  Teach students how to safely navigate busy sidewalks, intersections, and crosswalks.
  - Warn students not to take pictures with performers or costumed characters in Times Square.
  - Warn students not to hand their phones to strangers to take a picture of them. 
  - Caution students to be skeptical of unsolicited offers and interactions as they may be scam attempts.
  - Ask students to politely decline if they get asked for money by someone on the street or train. 
  - Ask students to avoid staring at anyone who may be exhibiting strange or extreme behavior and not take pictures. 

  
  

  - Navigation
      
      - At night and in the early morning, stick to main streets, avoid parks, poorly-lit areas, and places where you don’t see other people around.
      - If you feel uncomfortable or lost, approach a police officer, call your Senior Resident Assistant, or ask inside a store to get your bearings or directions. In the neighborhood around Columbia, there are “Safe Havens,” which are stores that have agreed to provide a secure place for students. You can identify these stores by the “Columbia Safe Haven” decal in their window, which features a red-colored lion. 
      - When in doubt of your destination or the safety of a neighborhood, take a cab – especially late at night. All New York City cabs accept credit cards.
      - Be prepared to switch routes or adjust plans if potential threats are identified at a location.
      - Keep group leaders at the front to scout the path and ensure route safety.

  
  

  - MTA/Subway 
      
      - If taking the subway late at night, stand near the "During off hours trains stop here" sign or in view of the Metro-Card booth. Ride in cars with more people and preferably in the conductor's car (you will see them look out the window of the train when it stops).
      - Avoid drawing attention to yourself as a tourist: Don't stand on street corners looking at maps and do your best to walk confidently. The best way to get this done is to take the time to plan and learn your route to your destination before you leave campus.
      - Coming back to Columbia, do not take the uptown 2/3 subway past 96th St., as the 2/3 trains do not go to Columbia University. Instead, at 96th St., transfer from the 2/3 to the 1 train, which stops right outside Columbia. The 116th stop on the 2/3 is a long way from the campus. Also, do not attempt to get to Columbia University by taking the A or C trains to 110 or 116, as those stops are quite far from campus.
      - Use caution when using ATMs and don't carry too much cash around with you -- most places accept credit cards and there are ATMs everywhere.
      - When using the subway at night, avoid the back of the train and aim for front or center cars closest to the conductors.
      -  If potential threats are encountered on the subway, switch cars or wait for the next train. Err on the side of caution.
      - Instruct students to stand well back from platform edges and curbs while waiting for trains and buses.

  

  - Harassment Prevention
      
      - Educate students on recognizing harassment and trusting their instincts.
      - Instruct students to avoid engaging with strangers who approach them.
      - Staff should scan the group frequently to look out for signs of harassment and intervene safely to move students away from potential harm.
      - Encourage students to report any uncomfortable situations to staff immediately.
  - Cultural Sensitivity and Respectful Behavior
      
      - Brief students on respectful behavior in diverse neighborhoods and cultural institutions.
      - Teach students about personal space norms in the city.
      - Advise on appropriate conversation volume levels in different settings.
      - Instruct students on respectful photography, especially in culturally sensitive areas.
  - Scams 
      
      - One common scam is strangers approaching you with a story about having difficulty getting home because they lost their wallet somehow and need money for train or bus fare. Tell these people you cannot help them.

  

  - Another scam involves people selling fake tickets to tourist attractions. Make sure you buy your tickets from official ticket windows and not from ticket agents on the sidewalks near the attractions. If you are unsure, find a police officer and ask where to buy tickets.

  

  - Safety Measures 
      
      - If a student or intern calls and wants to leave a place due to danger, the program and Public Safety of the university can do many things to help. Ask the students to call anytime they need help.
      - You do not have to talk with someone or be near someone who makes you uncomfortable. If you are uncomfortable, simply move. If someone is talking to you, simply say, “Sorry. I have to go.” And then leave.
      - During hot weather, schedule regular breaks in shaded or air-conditioned areas to prevent heat exhaustion. Always drink plenty of water\!

  

  - **Digital Safety**  
      - Advise students against sharing their location on social media during the trip.
      - Remind students not to connect to unsecured public Wi-Fi networks.
      - Encourage keeping phones in secure, zipped pockets or bags to prevent theft. Warn students not to keep phones and wallets in a back pocket.

  

  - **Emergency Response**

<!-- end list -->

  - Pause to assess the situation, then act to minimize further threats.
  - Call 911 if it's an emergency.
  - For issues on trains or buses, report to an MTA employee or police officer before leaving the station. Do not leave without making a report.
  - Stay on the scene to wait for the police to file a report.
  - Ensure all witnesses or involved parties are present.
  - Call the director on duty immediately. Don't wait until returning to campus.
  - File a maxient report for all off-campus incidents, big or small.

  

  - **Post-Trip Procedures**

<!-- end list -->

  -  Conduct a brief debrief with students to discuss any concerns or incidents, even minor ones.
  - Staff can share observations and suggestions with the administration for improving safety on future trips.
  - Remember, your primary responsibility is the safety and well-being of the students. Always err on the side of caution and don't hesitate to adjust plans if safety concerns arise.

# **Protection of Minors**

This Protection of Minors Policy creates Standards of Conduct for all interactions

between members of our community, and all volunteers, contractors, and consultants who interact with, supervise, chaperone, or otherwise oversee minors in programs or activities at the University. 

  

This policy creates an independent duty for those who interact with, supervise, chaperone, or otherwise oversee minors in University-sponsored programs, activities, and/or residential facilities to be trained by the University. It also requires an independent duty to report suspected cases of abuse and maltreatment of individuals under the age of 18 immediately. Any member of the University community may report a concern if they have reasonable suspicion that a child has been abused or maltreated, either by a caregiver at home, or by any other person they believe could be responsible for abuse.

  

If there is reasonable cause to believe that a minor may have been abused or maltreated by a parent or caregiver legally responsible for the child, a report will be submitted to the New York State Child Abuse & Maltreatment Hotline. 

  

## **Definitions of Child Abuse and Child Maltreatment**

  

**Child Abuse**- Generally, the term abuse encompasses the most serious harms committed against children. An "abused child" is a child whose parent or other person legally responsible for his/her care inflicts upon the child serious physical injury, creates a substantial risk of serious physical injury, or commits an act of sex abuse against the child. Not only can a person be abusive to a child if they perpetuate any of these actions against a child in their care, they can be guilty of abusing a child if they allow someone else to do these things to that child.

  

**Child Maltreatment-** Maltreatment refers to the quality of care a child is receiving from those responsible for him/her. Maltreatment occurs when a parent or other person legally responsible for the care of a child harms a child or places a child in imminent danger of harm by failing to exercise the minimum degree of care in providing the child with any of the following: food, clothing, shelter, education or medical care when financially able to do so. Maltreatment can also result from abandonment of a child or from not providing adequate supervision for the child. Further, a child may be maltreated if a parent engages in excessive use of drugs or alcohol such that it interferes with their ability to adequately supervise the child.

  

If you have a reasonable cause to suspect the abuse or maltreatment of a minor, you are legally required to report it. See the reporting protocol in the POM One-Pager.

## **Discrimination, Harassment, or Gender-Based Misconduct** 

Columbia University is committed to fostering an environment that is free from gender-based discrimination and harassment, including sexual assault and all other forms of gender-based misconduct. The University recognizes its responsibility to increase awareness of such misconduct, prevent its occurrence, diligently investigate reports of misconduct, support students and others who experience gender-based misconduct, and respond fairly and firmly when students violate University policy. The University is also committed to supporting students accused of gender-based misconduct who go through the disciplinary process. In addressing issues of gender-based misconduct, all members of the University must respect and care for one another in a manner consistent with our deeply held academic and community values. Here are several important points about gender-based misconduct that the Policy addresses: 

  

  - Sexual harassment, sexual assault, sexual exploitation, gender-based harassment, stalking, domestic violence, and dating violence are all forms of gender-based misconduct. In some instances, behavior that is not sexual in nature (including comments meant in jest) can be considered gender-based misconduct. 
  - Gender-based misconduct can occur between strangers or acquaintances, or people who know each other well, including between people involved in an intimate or sexual relationship.
  - Gender-based misconduct can be committed by anyone regardless of gender identity, and it can occur between people of the same or different sex or gender.

  

Students who believe they have been subjected to gender-based discrimination or harassment are encouraged to report these incidents and should immediately call the Program Office or report it to the Director of Student Affairs, High School & Non-Degree Programs. Program staff and all University employees are obligated to report any event of misconduct, harassment or discrimination of which they become aware. 

  

Once the Program receives a report of gender-based misconduct, the information will be provided to Columbia University Public Safety and Student Conduct and Community Standards. The Gender-Based Misconduct Office within Student Conduct and Community Standards (“the Office”) serves as the central point of contact for all University students affected by gender-based misconduct. After receiving a report, the Office will contact each party to discuss relevant options for resolution. The Office will make the final determination regarding the appropriate resolution process and can require an investigation even if the parties request to engage in a different resolution process. The Office also has the authority to take immediate and corrective action to address all alleged misconduct and any additional alleged Policy violations that have occurred. The Office, in consultation with the appropriate Title IX Coordinator(s), will determine if action is appropriate. If selected by the students and/or if the Office deems necessary, an investigation will be initiated. The Office will designate a two-person team (“the Investigative Team”) to conduct an investigation into whether a violation of the Policy occurred. All Title IX Investigators will have extensive training in investigating and evaluating conduct prohibited under the Policy. The Investigative Team will be impartial and unbiased. Moreover, due to the short duration of the Programs, the investigation may be appropriately expedited. 

  

When a student is found responsible a sanction will be imposed. Sanctions will be fair and appropriate, given the facts of a particular case; consistent with the University’s handling of similar cases; adequate to protect the safety of the campus community; and reflective of the seriousness of gender-based misconduct. Sanctions may include dismissal from the Program. In some cases, the New York State Child Abuse and Maltreatment Hotline may be contacted; which may lead to the local police being notified.

## **Examples of Gender-Based Misconduct**

Specific categories of gender-based misconduct and other important definitions used in this policy are included in the Definitions section following the Procedures, along with scenarios illustrating specific instances of gender-based misconduct. For purposes of illustration, the following list sets forth examples of conduct that could constitute gender-based misconduct under those definitions:

  - Coercion for a date or a romantic or intimate relationship
  - Unwelcome touching, kissing, hugging, or massaging
  - Use of unwanted force in connection with sexual activity or attempted sexual activity
  - Sexual contact with a person who has not clearly consented
  - Unwelcome remarks about the private parts of a person’s body
  - Belittling remarks about a person’s gender or sexual orientation based on gender-stereotyping
  - Videotaping or photographing of activity of a sexual or private nature without the consent of the person(s) being videotaped or photographed
  - Obscene gestures of a sexual or gender-based nature
  - Graffiti concerning the sexual activity of another person
  - Use of email, the Internet, or other forms of digital media to facilitate any of the behaviors listed above 

If you have a reasonable cause to suspect a student or other community member has experienced Gender-Based Misconduct, you are required to report it.

## **Active Shooter Incident**

The following guidelines are intended to reduce your personal risk in the unlikely event that an Active Shooter Incident should occur on campus. If you are outside a building when an event occurs, you should take immediate cover, preferably inside a building, circumstances permitting.

  

**If you are in a building when an event occurs, you should:** 

  

**Secure immediate area:**  

  - Lock and barricade doors
  - Do not stand by doors or windows
  - Turn off lights
  - Close blinds
  - Block windows
  - Turn off radios and computer monitors
  - Keep yourself out of sight and take adequate cover/protection (i.e., concrete walls, thick desks, filing cabinets - cover may protect you from bullets)

  

**Contacting Authorities:** 

  - 4-5555 from any CU Phone
  - Use Emergency 911
  - <publicsafety@columbia.edu>

  

Be aware that the 212-854-5555 number will likely be overwhelmed.  Program the alternate Columbia University Public Safety line (212-854-2797) into your cell phone for backup or consider 911 or e-mail.  Remember, most cell phones provide the ability to make an emergency call, even if they have a lockout feature.  E-mail may also be an option if, under the circumstances, you are unable to speak.  E-mail is monitored by Public Safety personnel.  

  

**What to Report:**  

  - Your specific location-building name and office/room number
  - Number of people at your specific location
  - Injuries-number injured, types of injuries
  - Assailant(s)-location, number of suspects, race/gender, clothing description, physical features, type of weapons (long gun or handgun), backpack, shooter's identity if known, explosions separate from gunfire, etc.

  - Objective is to engage assailant(s) immediately
  - Evacuate victims
  - Facilitate follow up medical care, interviews, counseling

  - If doubt exists for the safety of the individuals inside the room, the area should remain secured
  - Know all alternate exits in your building

During the course of the program, students who are living together or attending the same classes may come into conflict with one another.   - Prepare the location:

  - Write an IR 
  - Check in on the students within 24-48 hours to see how they are doing

# **Managing Students of Concern**

Oftentimes, we may encounter a student who is struggling and who may be exhibiting some concerning behaviors. It is our job as student support staff to ensure our students are safe and doing okay. It is important to look for social or emotional cues that may indicate a student in distress or a student suffering psychologically. 

  

Our goals as a team are to :

  - Provide a safe physical environment for members of the university community,
  - Provide a safe emotional environment for the university community, 
  - Promote peace of mind for friends and family of the university community, and
  - Encourage ongoing self-care methods through tailed referral support services

  

## **The Distressed Student**

You may become aware of students in distress in a variety of ways. Students may directly communicate their issues and concerns to you. This may occur in private or in front of others. You may also sense there is something amiss with a student on the basis of the behavior they exhibit or on the basis of some changes in the demeanor over time.

  

## **The Many Signs of Distress**

As faculty and staff you may be the first to notice a student who is experiencing difficulty. In these situations, you do not have to take on the role of a counselor or attempt to diagnose a student. You need only to notice the signs of distress and communicate those to the appropriate resource. In some instances you may choose to have a direct conversation with the student to express your concerns and offer resource referral information. We will discuss these options in more detail below. 

  

Often there are indicators that a student is experiencing distress long before a situation escalates to a crisis. To assist our students in maintaining their well being and maximizing their intellectual growth, it is important to identify difficulties as early as possible. The presence of one of the following indicators alone does not necessarily mean the student is experiencing severe distress. However, the more indicators you notice, the more likely it is that the student may need help. 

  

**Academic Indicators**

  - Repeated absences from class, section, lab or employment
  - Missed assignments, exams or appointments
  - Deterioration in quality or quantity of work
  - Extreme disorganization or erratic performance
  - Written or artisitc expression of unusual violence, morbidity, social isolation, despair or confusion; essays or papers that docus on suicide or death that does not pertain to course topics
  - Continual seeking of special provisions (extensions on papers or deadlines, make-up exams)
  - Patterns of perfectionism; e.g. can’t accept themselves fully if they don’t get an A
  - Overblown or disproportionate response to grades or other evaluations 
  - Making inappropriate or off handed contributions to class discussions

  

**Behavioral and Emotional Indicators**

  - Direct statements indicating:
      
      - General distress
      - Family conflict
      - Grief & loss
      - Experience of discrimination, harrassment or sexual assault
      - Economic hardships
  - Angry or hostile outbursts, yelling or aggressive comments
  - Changes to physical appearance or personal hygiene
  - Unusual withdrawal or animated behavior
  - Expressions of hopelessness or worthlessness; crying or tearfulness
  - Expressions of severe anxiety or irritability
  - Excessively demanding or dependent behavior
  - Lack of response to outreach from course staff
  - Shakiness, tremors, fidgeting or pacing 

  

**Physical Indicators**

  - Deterioration in physical appearance or personal hygiene
  - Excessive fatigue, exhaustion; falling asleep in class repeatedly
  - Visible changes in weight; statements about change in appetite or sleep 
  - Noticeable cuts, bruises or burns
  - Frequent or chronic illness
  - Disorganized speech, rapid or slurred speech, confusion
  - Unusual inability to make eye contact
  - Coming to class bleary-eyed or smelling of alcohol or other substances

  

**Other Factors**

  - Concern about a student expressed by their peers, roommates or teaching assistant
  - A hunch or gut-level reaction that something is wrong

  

**DO….**

  - Express compassion without intimate friendship
  - Communicate your concern, if appropriate
  - Be specific and clear regarding the standards of behavior you expect if the students behaviors are interfering with classroom activities
  - Share your intention of referring them for assistance if applicable 
  - In cases of psychological or medical emergencies always contact Public Safety 

  

**AVOID…**

  - Taking responsibility for the student's emotional state by saying things such as “I’ll take care of it for you…” 
  - Discounting their distress by saying “It's not really that bad”
  - Assuring the student that you are their friend
  - Be overly warm and nurturing 
  - Challenging or agreeing with any mistaken or illogical beliefs
  - Saying anything else that might discount the personal significance or intensity of the students feelings
  - Agreeing to maintain student confidentiality 

  

**The Disruptive Student**

  

Often, large amounts of time and energy are given to students who make excessive demands on your time. These students may also have difficulty keeping their comments consistent with the activities or with the topic being discussed. Disruptive behavior interferes with the academic mission of the University or compromises the well-being of the University community. This includes but is not limited to behavior which is disruptive to the classroom or laboratory environment. Students should not expose others to conduct that is disorderly, lewd, or indecent.

Student Conduct and Community Standards provides detailed explanation and examples of disruptive and inappropriate behaviors. 

  

**DO…**

  - Remain calm and professional. Be sensitive to both verbal and nonverbal cues exhibited by a student who seems to be verbally aggressive. 
  - Intervene as soon as you hear or see the undesirable behavior. Be consistent with your intervention. 
  - Briefly and directly convey to the student that verbally aggressive behavior is unacceptable and that the matter must be resolved
  - If, in your best judgment, the verbally aggressive student threatens to create a safety risk, **contact Public Safety or NYPD immediately.** Use a phone out of sight/hearing of the individual is possible or request that someone else make the call. Report details of the incident
  - Consult with SPS Care Team and administrative staff for guidance and support 

  

**AVOID..**

  - Waiting until you have become too involved to seek assistance
  - Letting student use you as their only source of support
  - Giving direct advice “Why don’t you …. Etc.?”
  - Getting defensive
  - Engaging in a power struggle with the student
  - Responding with anger 

  

## **Student Emotional Distress**

As a member of the IBC, you may play a vital role in being among the first to recognize someone in deep emotional distress. In fact, we will have training on this topic.

  

The number one rule to remember when you encounter a student with emotional issues is that: 

YOU CANNOT HELP THE STUDENT ON YOUR OWN. The most caring thing you can do is reach out to administrators for help.

  

If a student appears to be in immediate danger, call either Public Safety (MS: (212) 854-5555 or dial 911.

  

Otherwise, if a student is expressing ambivalence about their emotional well-being, tells you that they are depressed, has big personal / identity issues they are struggling with, describes a re-emergent emotional crisis from the past etc., please console the student then reach out to the Wellness Coordinator right away, and submit a Maxient Report. If needed, the Wellness Coordinator will get the student in contact with the Wellness Counselor. 

  

Please do not try to help the student by yourself. Reporting such situations gives the student the best care, in which you might play a big part. 

  
Be wary of slipping into a “counseling” role as a student or a fellow intern. It can be tricky to know where the line is between being supportive and counseling / over your head, but here are some basic indicators:  
  

  - The individual is crying inconsolably or distressed;
  - The support conversation moves past 15 minutes;
  - The conversation doesn’t seem to progress to a sense of resolution;
  - The conversation is repeated nightly or frequently.

Suicidal ideation (thoughts of suicide) in children and teenagers often coincides with stressful life events. Keep in mind that what a young person sees as serious and insurmountable may seem minor to an adult — such as problems in school or the loss of a friendship. Despite this, do not minimize the pain they feel in relation to these. In some cases, a child or teen may feel suicidal due to certain life circumstances they may not want to talk about. Some of these include: 

  - Having a psychiatric disorder, including depression 
  - Loss or conflict with close friends or family members 
  - History of physical or sexual abuse 
  - Problems with alcohol or drugs 
  - Becoming pregnant 
  - Having a sexually transmitted infection 
  - Being the victim of bullying 
  - Being uncertain of sexual orientation 

Since the most frequent underlying cause of suicide is a psychiatric condition that requires professional intervention, we suggest you contact the Resident Director (RD), Wellness Counselor, or Assistant Dean Phong Luu if you have significant concerns about a student's well-being, together you can access professional help.

  

Responding to someone who appears troubled can be challenging. The simplest and best advice is to take any concerns about suicide very seriously. Do not be reluctant to talk about suicide openly and directly if a student makes suicidal references. At the same time, avoid offering assurances of complete confidentiality that you may not be able to honor, and avoid putting yourself in a situation in which you become the student's main source of support.

  

There is no simple formula for determining if someone is severely depressed and/or at risk for suicide, but there are behavioral "red flags" that may warrant seeking professional assistance.

  
**The more signs that are present, the greater the cause for concern. Among the indicators of possible risk are:**

  

  - Suicidal thoughts, even if indirect. "I don't ever want to wake up  
    again" "Everyone would be better off if I just died"

<!-- end list -->

  - Talking about "not being around" or about death. "What's the  
    difference? I won't be here anymore for finals." Giving away personal  
    possessions.
  - Extremely depressed mood, feelings of hopelessness, despair. "I feel like I'm in a hole and I can never get out. Things will never change."
  - Expressions of helplessness, loss of personal agency
  - Dramatic changes in personality, presentation, hygiene, or  
    functioning. 
  - High levels of anger, aggression or, alternatively, flattening of emotional expression, profound indifference
  - Reckless, risky or impulsive behavior
  - Thinking not grounded in reality; delusional beliefs
  - Very diminished self-esteem; chronic feelings of guilt,  
    worthlessness

  

**These behavioral indicators take on added urgency in certain contexts,****  
****among them:**

  

  - A past history of suicide attempts. Past attempts greatly increase the likelihood of future attempts. Therefore, if you are aware that someone has made suicide attempts in the past, suicidal ideation or a cluster of the other behavioral indicators listed above, it is that much more concerning.
  - Access to lethal means: Access to firearms greatly increases the likelihood of suicide. Attempts to obtain any lethal means (e.g., stockpiling pills) should be taken very seriously
  - Family history of suicide
  - Social isolation, lack of support network
  - Recent increase in life stressors may increase risk. Examples of stressors are the death of a loved one, romantic break-up, financial setbacks, legal problems, academic problems, serious health problems.

 

Stress, in and of itself, is not likely to result in suicidal behaviors; people weather these sorts of stressors all the time. However, stress may precipitate suicidal behaviors in someone who is already vulnerable.

  

It is obviously not your role to undertake a clinical assessment. However, you can be of great service by recognizing a student's distress and facilitating a referral for treatment. The Wellness Team, Counseling and Psychological Services and the CUMC Student Mental Health Service are available to our program to act as a consultant to anyone in the community worried about a student. Please do not hesitate to contact them through our Wellness Counselor or Assistant Dean Phong Luu.

  
  

## **Obtaining Health Care / Emergencies**

## **Columbia University Health Services** 

Health Services, located on the 3rd floor of John Jay Hall, is open Monday through Thursday from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM and Friday from 9:00 am to 4:00 pm.   
  

Health Services will see urgent care patients without an appointment. 

  

For non-urgent needs (for example, something is bothering you or the student, but it can wait until tomorrow), it is best to make an appointment.  Call Health Services (212-854-2284) to make an appointment.

  

Health Services provides (related to Urgent Care) examinations, guidance and prescriptions for free. Further testing or blood work is paid by the intern/student and/or insurance.

  

Students must be accompanied by their RA or another program assistant when going to Health Services. Inform the receptionist at Health Services that you are with “the Columbia University Pre-College Program.”  
  

Students will often put off reporting they are ill until after class or even until curfew (i.e., after obligations and fun are over). **But waiting until the evening means a hospital emergency room visit, which will take a lot more time and last most of the night.** Encourage your students to report whenever they feel unwell – there are lots of times during the day for a Health Services visit when they won’t also miss class or events.

  

Also, if students tell you they are too ill to go to class, they must go to Health Services.  
  

Any questions or concerns about Health Services should be directed to Assistant Director Jeannette Sanchez.

  

**Health & Chaperones****

Be sure to check in with each student and make a plan with them to make sure they address any of their health needs should they disclose upon arrival.

**Dehydration & Heat**

  

New York City summers are very hot, and each summer features a student or two fainting from heat exhaustion. This can be very serious if it happens away from a program setting, so we want the students to be very aware of how to protect themselves in extreme heat.

  

Promote water drinking among your students. Educate them about heat in the city; ask them to imagine passing-out on a subway. When running activities, take frequent breaks and be sure students (and you) are well-hydrated. Be really strict about this, as kids think they’re invincible, so if you have to force the activity to cease and the students to drink, do it.

  

It is inadvisable to have strenuous exercise outdoors in the heat. If you or students are keen for outdoor exercise, try to do so in the morning or evenings when it is cooler. Runners should form running clubs to head out together early or late.

  

**Allergies**

  

Food allergies are especially common these days, but other allergies to be acutely aware of might be allergies to wasps and bees, medications etc.

  

Look over your students’ health forms and take time to discover any allergies your students have, what sort of reactions they have, how they treat them and what sort of response you can provide.

  

If the allergy is related to food, ensure other students are aware of necessary safety precautions.

  

**Sexual Health & Condoms**

  

While RAs are not permitted to distribute condoms to students, they should inform students that a safe sex / sexual health / plan-b vending machine is located in the lobby of John Jay Hall. Also free condoms are available at Health Services and outside the elevator on the 8th floor of Lerner Hall.

# **Intern Self Care** 

This self-care section is included at the direct request of past interns. The fact is that the IBC is a physically demanding experience, and to keep up energy and health, interns must be attentive to getting proper sleep, eating well, and pacing themselves. 

  

The program is very busy, and it’s also very fun. It is very easy to forgo an hour nap because there is definitely something going on. But that extra sleep now can make the later weeks of the IBC more enjoyable. It can also help you avoid a visit to the doctor.

  

Consider:

The IBC starts with \~60 hours of seminars packed with information to learn/master, many more hours of program work and preparation, lots of socializing with new IBC friends, lots of time in the city (all of it walking and walking), a moving day, a 15+ hour workday for residential student check-in, followed by a 6am wake-up for a 17+ hour workday the first day of classes. 

  

Then there will be still six weeks to go… at a similar pace… in the NYC summer heat.

  

Again, interns need to be deliberately attentive to sleep, eating, and pacing. Below are some suggestions and strategies for self-care, but also talk to your Senior Resident Assistant (SRA) about how they plan to keep up their energy.

  

## **Self-Care Strategies**

1.  Sleep 
      
    1.  Shoot for 8 hours nightly. Consider the next morning and do the math. Keep that goal in mind as you plan and go about your evening.
    2.  Designate two weeknights each week as ‘go-to-bed-at-11:30pm nights’.
    3.  There is some downtime in the IBC, make it a goal to take 5 naps per week. 

  

1.  Eat well
      
    1.  While Ferris Booth has lots of pizza, soda and fried delights, there are also plenty of healthier options. Make it a point to have fruit with breakfast, salads each day, and aim for the healthier options as much as your will-power allows.

  

1.  Hydrate
      
    1.  It will be hot; drink lots of liquids. Avoid sugary soda and sports drinks and go for water. Hydrate well even on cooler days.

  

1.  Downtime / Realistic scheduling
      
    1.  Yes, you should be busy, but we also want you to be happy and capable. A normal schedule sees an intern fulfilling their RA duties, running a trip each week, and being generally helpful to and engaged with the community. 
    2.  Do not run a late night Cultural Excursion followed by an early morning. 
    3.  Similarly, social interaction is physically and mentally draining. Be sure to give yourself a mental break now-and-then via some alone time.

  

1.  Keep doing what you do.
      
    1.  Do you go to the gym often? Don’t stop because you feel tired, skipping your work-out will make you feel more sluggish. Do you read a lot? Find time to keep doing so. Play Frisbee, go running, play basketball, knit, manage your fantasy baseball team, do yoga, write your poems, etc. These activities will help you decompress and sustain your physical and mental energy.

  

1.  Avoid getting drunk when off-duty.
      
    1.  It’s useful to believe a drunk person needs to sleep once for alcohol and once for normal sleep. If you get drunk on your nights-off, it is like missing a whole night of sleep. 

## **Ability to Thrive**

The IBC wants to support you and help you have a successful, fulfilling summer. If you are struggling with any personal or health problems, please know that you can talk with your Senior, RD, Jeannette or Phong about your needs. When we have information from you, we can then best help you.

  

Things to consider:  
  

  - You should not feel compelled to do work you are not physically capable of, either due to disability or illness.
  - Taking care of your mental health is just as important as your physical health. If you are feeling overwhelmed, anxious, or emotionally exhausted, please speak to someone–you do not have to manage this alone.
  - Sleep, nutrition, and hydration matter. If your schedule is interfering with your ability to meet these basic needs, let us know.
  - Personal identities and lived experiences impact how you navigate work. If you ever feel unsafe, unseen, or unsupported, we want to hear from you.
  - If you’re unsure whether what you're experiencing is something you should bring up, err on the side of checking in. There’s no harm in starting the conversation.
  - We would rather you communicate your needs than push through in silence.  
      

We understand you have a life beyond the IBC that can impact your engagement. We also know that you will have burn-out moments while with us. All those things are normal\! If you are in need of rest or additional support at any time, reach out and we will do our best to help you.
